On January 20, 2026, I pushed the first commit: 56 files, 8,305 lines, zero dependencies, and bilingual from the jump. The core idea? Grab [LinkedIn](https://linkedin.com)'s messy CSV export and turn it into clean [Markdown](https://daringfireball.net/projects/markdown/) that an [LLM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_language_model) can actually digest to make sense of your career history. I built the whole thing in a single day—22 commits across roughly seven hours.

## The Origin Story

This tool didn't happen in a vacuum. I built it right alongside two sister utilities—[github2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/github2md) and [gitlab2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/gitlab2md)—simply because I needed them for another project I was (and still am) deep into: [fu7ur3pr00f](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/fu7ur3pr00f). I coded them, pushed them live, and honestly just moved on to the next thing.

Half a year and [10 releases](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/releases) later, the stats are modest, completely organic, and totally independent:

## The Behind-the-Scenes Reality

**Bilingual by Design:** The `--lang es` flag was hardwired from that very first commit. Making it international wasn't an afterthought or a "nice-to-have"; it was a base requirement.

**LLMs First:** Just 38 minutes after the initial commit, [NotebookLM](https://notebooklm.google.com) was explicitly named in the README as the main use case, even before [Claude](https://claude.ai) or [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md). The intention was crystal clear from day one: Markdown is the absolute best format for an LLM to read.

**Pivoting Fast:** Switched the license from [MIT](https://opensource.org/licenses/MIT) to [GPL-2.0](https://www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl-2.0.html) on the first day. Within 90 minutes of launching, [`pipx`](https://pipx.pypa.io) became the go-to installation method.

**Four Months of Radio Silence:** Not a single commit between late January and late May. I wasn't tracking it at all. I had shipped it, shifted my focus, and genuinely forgot to check the repo.

**The May Revival & Community:** On May 22, I was messing around with some coding agents—first [OpenClaw](https://openclaw.ai), then [Hermes](https://hermes-agent.nousresearch.com)—and had one of them run a routine scan across my active projects. It came back with a number that made no sense: 5,100 downloads. For a script I had thrown together in an afternoon and abandoned. I told the agent to double-check. Same result. That afternoon, I finally opened the repo back up, read through the issue tracker, and started coding on it for the first time in four months.

That same burst of motivation spawned the PDF resume generator via the `--pdf` flag. And over the next 48 hours, the project got its first external PRs from devs I've never met: [Viswanatha](https://github.com/blinkerbit) (from India) and [nanookclaw](https://github.com/nanookclaw). They jumped in and added a [progress spinner](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/pull/8) and a user-requested [`--version` flag](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues/4).

I don't know these guys. We've never talked. They stumbled onto the repo, fixed a problem, and left. A few years ago, that would be the end of the story. But today, the reality is I don't even know if they wrote those PRs themselves or if an AI agent did it for them. I use agents every single day for my own dev work; I'd be kidding myself to think I'm the only one. A massive chunk of code is AI-assisted now, meaning a percentage of open-source contributions are entirely agent-mediated, and nobody is really tracking it.

Honestly? I don't think it matters. What matters is that someone, somewhere, cared enough about [a niche CLI tool for LinkedIn exports](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md) to spend their attention on it—whether it was their own attention or their agent's.

**Tooling Experiments:** I threw in [`uv`](https://docs.astral.sh/uv/) dependency groups on May 22 and ripped them right back out 24 hours later to keep the footprint lean. Complicated tooling usually has a short lifespan.

**What Stuck:** The default output directory (`linkedin_export`), the `--lang es` flag, and the core philosophy: turn a ZIP into Markdown. Everything else is just an optional flag.

## What's Next?

The [issue tracker](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues) has 11 open tickets, mostly tagged [`good first issue`](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues?q=is%3Aissue+is%3Aopen+label%3A%22good+first+issue%22) and [`help wanted`](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues?q=is%3Aissue+is%3Aopen+label%3A%22help+wanted%22). The codebase is intentionally small—any dev could jump in, read it, and ship a PR in a single afternoon.

The immediate roadmap is strictly about practical, no-BS utility:

- [`--format json`](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues?q=is%3Aissue+json) to plug into data pipelines.
- Cleaning up the aesthetics of the [PDF layout](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues?q=is%3Aissue+pdf).

What's **not** happening: No AI agent integrations, no pivot to a SaaS model, no "Pro" tier paywall. It's just [a CLI](https://pypi.org/project/linkedin2md/) that does one thing well and stays out of your way.

## Why This Matters

Watching this project grow organically proves exactly why I like building this way: small, hyper-focused, single-author software is alive and kicking. You don't need a massive team, VC funding, or a hyped-up marketing launch on TechCrunch.

You just need to solve your own headache well enough that other people dealing with the exact same headache can find your code. And then, you just keep showing up—one [issue](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues) and one [PR](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/pulls) at a time.

Turns out, that actually works.
