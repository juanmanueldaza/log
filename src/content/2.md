# linkedin2md, Six Months In

On January 20, 2026, [`linkedin2md`](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md) shipped its first commit: 56 files, 8,305 lines, zero dependencies, and bilingual from day one. The idea: turn LinkedIn's messy CSV export into Markdown an LLM can read. It was built in a single day — 22 commits across about seven hours.

Six months later:

```
Stars:          18
Forks:           3
Open issues:    11
PyPI downloads: 5,100 (~1,600 unique IPs, ~38/day)
Latest:         v0.3.1 (May 23)
Commits:        43
Contributors:   3
License:        GPL-2.0
Dependencies:   0 (core); weasyprint + markdown (PDF only, conditional)
```

None of those numbers break anything. They're just real.

## How it actually went

The initial commit had `--lang es` wired in from the start. The problem wasn't "how do I add Spanish later" — it was never monolingual to begin with. The design assumed internationalization from commit #1.

Thirty-eight minutes after the initial commit, NotebookLM appeared in the README as the first listed LLM use case, above Claude and Obsidian. The commit message: *"Highlight Markdown as ideal format for LLM analysis."* That positioning was intentional from the second commit, not discovered by accident.

The license started as MIT and was changed to GPL-2.0 later that same day. `pipx` became the recommended install method at v0.1.2, about 90 minutes after the project went public.

Then four months of quiet. No commits between late January and late May.

On May 22, the PDF resume generator landed — a `--pdf` flag that conditionally imports weasyprint and markdown. The dependencies stay at zero unless you ask for a CV. The next two days brought the first external contributions: a progress spinner and bug fixes from two new contributors who found the project and shipped PRs. A few days later, `--version` landed from an issue filed by a user.

Also on May 23: `uv` dependency groups, added the day before, were removed entirely. The commit: *"fix: remove uv-specific dependency-groups, add uv.lock to .gitignore."* Complicated tooling, 24-hour lifespan.

## What stayed the same

The output directory default is `linkedin_export` — unchanged since the initial commit. Bilingual support is still `--lang es`, same as day one. The tool still does one thing: convert a ZIP to Markdown. Everything else is a flag.

## What the issue tracker says

11 open issues. Most are tagged `good first issue` and `help wanted`. The top requests are `--format json` for data pipelines and better PDF aesthetics. The project is deliberately scoped small enough that a newcomer can read the codebase and ship a PR in an afternoon.

## The numbers at scale

5,100 downloads over 134 days averages ~38/day. 1,605 unique IPs — the rest are mirrors and CI. The v0.3.1 release day spiked to 319; the following 10 days averaged 52/day. The trajectory is slow and real.

Three contributors. Two of them showed up in month four. Forty-three commits. Zero marketing.

## What's next

Two things the issue tracker points to: `--format json` for pipeline integration, and better PDF layout. No agent integrations, no SaaS pivot, no "Pro" tier. A CLI that does one thing well and stays out of the way.

## Why this matters

The point of `linkedin2md` — and the rest of the tools on [my GitHub](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza) — is that small, focused, single-author software is still viable. You don't need a team, a marketing budget, or a launch.

You need to solve your own problem well enough that others who share it can find you. Then keep showing up, one issue at a time, for as long as you can give it attention.

Turns out that works.
