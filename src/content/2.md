# linkedin2md: Six Months In

_Posted on: June 03, 2026_

On January 20, 2026, linkedin2md shipped its first commit: 56 files, 8,305 lines, zero dependencies, and bilingual from day one. The core idea? Turn LinkedIn's messy CSV export into clean Markdown that an LLM can actually digest to understand your career history. It was built in a single day—22 commits across about seven hours.

## The Origin Story

The tool wasn't born in isolation. It was built in parallel with two sister utilities—[github2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/github2md) and [gitlab2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/gitlab2md)—simply because I needed them for another project I was (and still am) deep into: [fu7ur3pr00f](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/fu7ur3pr00f). I built them, published them, and initially just moved on.

Six months and 10 releases later, the stats are modest, real, and independent:

## How It Actually Went

**Bilingual by Design:** The initial commit had `--lang es` wired in from the start. Internationalization wasn't an afterthought; it was a core requirement.

**The LLM Focus:** Just 38 minutes after the first commit, NotebookLM was added to the README as the primary use case, ahead of Claude and Obsidian. The intent was clear from day one: Markdown is the ideal format for LLM analysis.

**Rapid Early Pivots:** The license shifted from MIT to GPL-2.0 on day one. Within 90 minutes of going public, `pipx` became the recommended installation method.

**The Four-Month Silence:** Zero commits between late January and late May. Just quiet utility while it did its job.

**The May Revival & Community:** On May 22, the PDF resume generator landed via the `--pdf` flag. The next two days brought the project's first external contributions from people I've never met face-to-face: Viswanatha (from India) and nanookclaw. They shipped PRs to add a progress spinner and a user-requested `--version` flag.

**Tooling Experiments:** `uv` dependency groups were added on May 22 and completely stripped out 24 hours later to keep things lean. Complicated tooling, short lifespan.

**What stayed the same:** The default output directory (`linkedin_export`), the `--lang es` flag, and the core philosophy: convert a ZIP to Markdown. Everything else is just an optional flag.

## What's Next?

The issue tracker has 11 open issues, mostly tagged `good first issue` and `help wanted`. The codebase is intentionally small enough that a newcomer can easily read it and ship a PR in an afternoon.

The immediate roadmap is driven strictly by practical utility:

- `--format json` for data pipelines.
- Better PDF layout aesthetics.

What's **not** happening: No AI agent integrations, no SaaS pivot, no "Pro" tier. Just a CLI that does one thing well and stays out of the way.

## Why This Matters

The journey of linkedin2md reinforces why I build this way: small, focused, single-author software is still entirely viable. You don't need a massive team, a venture budget, or a coordinated marketing launch.

You just need to solve your own problem well enough that others who share it can find your tools. Then, you just keep showing up—one issue and one PR at a time.

Turns out, that works.
