# [linkedin2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md): Six Months In

_Posted on: June 03, 2026_

On January 20, 2026, [linkedin2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md) shipped its first commit: 56 files, 8,305 lines, zero dependencies, and bilingual from day one. The core idea? Turn [LinkedIn](https://linkedin.com)'s messy CSV export into clean [Markdown](https://daringfireball.net/projects/markdown/) that an [LLM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_language_model) can actually digest to understand your career history. It was built in a single day—22 commits across about seven hours.

## The Origin Story

The tool wasn't born in isolation. It was built in parallel with two sister utilities—[github2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/github2md) and [gitlab2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/gitlab2md)—simply because I needed them for another project I was (and still am) deep into: [fu7ur3pr00f](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/fu7ur3pr00f). I built them, published them, and initially just moved on.

Six months and [10 releases](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/releases) later, the stats are modest, real, and independent:

## How It Actually Went

**Bilingual by Design:** The initial commit had `--lang es` wired in from the start. Internationalization wasn't an afterthought; it was a core requirement.

**The LLM Focus:** Just 38 minutes after the first commit, [NotebookLM](https://notebooklm.google.com) was added to the README as the primary use case, ahead of [Claude](https://claude.ai) and [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md). The intent was clear from day one: Markdown is the ideal format for LLM analysis.

**Rapid Early Pivots:** The license shifted from [MIT](https://opensource.org/licenses/MIT) to [GPL-2.0](https://www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl-2.0.html) on day one. Within 90 minutes of going public, [`pipx`](https://pipx.pypa.io) became the recommended installation method.

**The Four-Month Silence:** Zero commits between late January and late May. I wasn't watching. I'd shipped it, moved on to other things, and honestly forgot to check back.

**The May Revival & Community:** On May 22, I was experimenting with coding agents — first [OpenCode](https://opencode.ai), then [Hermes](https://hermes-agent.nousresearch.com) — and had one of them do some routine research across my projects. It came back with a number I didn't expect: 5,100 downloads. For a tool I'd built in a day and abandoned. I asked it to double-check. Same number. That afternoon I opened the repo again, read through the issues, and started working on it for the first time in four months.

That same burst of attention produced the PDF resume generator via the `--pdf` flag. The next two days brought the project's first external contributions from people I've never met face-to-face: [Viswanatha](https://github.com/blinkerbit) (from India) and [nanookclaw](https://github.com/nanookclaw). They shipped PRs to add a [progress spinner](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/pull/8) and a user-requested [`--version` flag](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues/4).

I don't know these people. We've never spoken. They found the repo, fixed something, and moved on. That used to be the whole story. But I also don't know if they wrote those PRs themselves or if an agent did — because I use agents every day for my own work, and I'd be naive to assume I'm the only one. A growing share of code is AI-assisted now. Some percentage of open source contributions are agent-mediated, and nobody's tracking how much. I'm not sure it matters. What matters is that someone, somewhere, cared enough about [a CLI tool for LinkedIn exports](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md) to spend attention on it — whether their own or their agent's.

**Tooling Experiments:** [`uv`](https://docs.astral.sh/uv/) dependency groups were added on May 22 and completely stripped out 24 hours later to keep things lean. Complicated tooling, short lifespan.

**What stayed the same:** The default output directory (`linkedin_export`), the `--lang es` flag, and the core philosophy: convert a ZIP to Markdown. Everything else is just an optional flag.

## What's Next?

The [issue tracker](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues) has 11 open issues, mostly tagged [`good first issue`](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues?q=is%3Aissue+is%3Aopen+label%3A%22good+first+issue%22) and [`help wanted`](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues?q=is%3Aissue+is%3Aopen+label%3A%22help+wanted%22). The codebase is intentionally small enough that a newcomer can easily read it and ship a PR in an afternoon.

The immediate roadmap is driven strictly by practical utility:

- [`--format json`](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues?q=is%3Aissue+json) for data pipelines.
- Better [PDF layout aesthetics](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues?q=is%3Aissue+pdf).

What's **not** happening: No AI agent integrations, no SaaS pivot, no "Pro" tier. Just [a CLI](https://pypi.org/project/linkedin2md/) that does one thing well and stays out of the way.

## Why This Matters

The journey of [linkedin2md](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md) reinforces why I build this way: small, focused, single-author software is still entirely viable. You don't need a massive team, a venture budget, or a coordinated marketing launch.

You just need to solve your own problem well enough that others who share it can find your tools. Then, you just keep showing up—one [issue](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/issues) and one [PR](https://github.com/juanmanueldaza/linkedin2md/pulls) at a time.

Turns out, that works.
